📌 Strategy Overview

Weather Trader uses official forecast data to compare model-informed probabilities with market prices. Weather markets are appealing because the outcome definition is usually clear and the holding period is short.

💡 Core idea: when forecast confidence and market pricing diverge by enough to cover costs and noise, the gap may be tradable.
WeatherNOAAData Edge

🔄 Core Logic

1. Data source

NOAA Forecasts

Official weather projections for temperature, precipitation, snowfall, and related variables.

Time horizon

Best used on short forecast windows where model reliability is higher.

City coverage

Works best in liquid metro-based weather markets with stable public data.

2. Opportunity detection

  • Scan active markets: focus on clearly defined weather contracts
  • Compare forecasts to price: treat market price as implied probability
  • Require a threshold: only act when the gap is large enough to matter
  • Recheck timing: weather forecasts update often, so stale inputs can be dangerous

3. Execution

  • Size by confidence: larger only when the forecast edge is clearer
  • Diversify across markets: avoid putting all exposure into one event
  • Prefer short hold times: many weather markets resolve within days, not months

⚙️ What You Need

  • Data: NOAA or other trustworthy weather forecast sources
  • Workflow: regular scans plus a clean method for comparing forecast probability and market price
  • Execution: enough liquidity to enter without destroying the edge
  • Practice: use paper-trading or Simmer before committing real capital

📊 Advantages & Risks

Advantages

Reliable Public Data

Forecast inputs are transparent and easy to audit.

Low Narrative Noise

Weather markets are often less crowded by political or social-media interpretation.

Fast Capital Recycling

Many contracts resolve quickly, reducing long exposure duration.

Risks

  • Forecast uncertainty: extreme or rapidly changing weather can break assumptions
  • Thin liquidity: some weather markets are too shallow to trade efficiently
  • Model overconfidence: a good forecast source does not mean every gap is real edge
