Conviction Score & Copytrading

Updated: 2026-03-11 · Source synthesis: MeetPolyHub

Core idea

Blindly copying profitable wallets often underperforms because you are not copying only their best ideas. You are copying their entire behavior: probes, hedges, low-conviction entries, and true high-confidence trades.

Key insight: Copying smart money is not replication. It is translation. You need to infer how serious the trader is about a position.

Why blind copytrading fails

  • Probe trades: small entries used to test market conditions.
  • Hedge trades: risk-management positions that may not reflect directional conviction.
  • Noise trades: exploratory or low-certainty bets.
  • High-conviction trades: the rare positions where real size and repeated adds show stronger belief.

What conviction score tries to measure

Conviction score estimates how strongly a trader believes in a trade by looking at behavioral clues:

  • Bet size relative to wallet size
  • Repeated adds in the same market
  • Historical quality of similar signals
  • Context around timing and trade concentration

The trade-off is straightforward: stricter filters improve average quality, but reduce opportunity count.

How to use it in practice

  • 0–0.2: higher flow, higher noise, useful for portfolio replication.
  • 0.2–0.4: balanced default for most users.
  • 0.4–0.6: fewer trades, better marginal efficiency, suited for small capital and higher selectivity.

pred101 takeaway

If you want a usable copytrading framework, rank traders first, then filter their trades by conviction. Wallet discovery is only step one. Trade-quality inference is where the edge begins.
